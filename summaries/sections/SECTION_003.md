# SUMMARY: SECTION_003
## Undated — Letter to Tony Hiss (*The Real World*)

Dick writes to Tony Hiss at *The Real World* magazine, enclosing a commissioned piece about Tony Boucher. The cover letter is characteristically playful—joking about abbreviating his novel titles ("I sure think CRAP is wonderful") and threatening Hiss if the piece isn't used. But the enclosed essay is a deeply moving memorial to Tony Boucher, Dick's first editor and writing teacher, who died of cancer. 

Dick narrates Boucher's paradox: a man who loved the universe but was terrified of it, who stayed at his post despite his fear. He recounts Boucher's writing class ($1/night), his first story sale ("Roog"), and how Boucher was the only person who understood the story. Dick then makes a startling metempsychotic claim: his cat Pinky, who died of cancer at age 3, *was* Tony Boucher reincarnated. Evidence: Pinky's asthma (like Boucher's), his fear, his gentleness, and a dream in which a great tiger emerged from a "light-struck glade"—Tony the Tiger. After Pinky's death, Dick felt a consoling hand on his shoulder from an invisible presence and dreamed of album notes for "Don Pasquale" with five strings of catgut—a final hello from Pinky/Boucher.

## key_claims
- Tony Boucher was a man defined by the paradox of loving the universe and being terrified by it.
- Pinky the cat was Tony Boucher reincarnated ("served up by the universe again").
- The "Roogs" (garbagemen in his first story) represent something that "comes and eats up people who are sleeping in tranquility."

## recurring_concepts
- Metempsychosis / Reincarnation (Boucher → Pinky)
- Fear and courage as spiritual complementaries
- The "Roog" archetype: predatory forces disguised as mundane agents

## scholarly_value
This section is essential for understanding Dick's concept of metempsychosis and how he maps personal grief (the death of his cat) onto theological cosmology. The Boucher memorial also demonstrates his ability to move between registers—from slapstick comedy to genuinely poignant elegy—within a single text. The "Roog" interpretation retroactively re-reads his earliest fiction as prophetic.
