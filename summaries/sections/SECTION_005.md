# SUMMARY: SECTION_005
## March 5, 1975 — Letter to Ursula K. Le Guin

A remarkable letter to Ursula K. Le Guin, one of the most fully developed theoretical statements of Dick's 1974/1975 cosmology. Dick proposes that he and Le Guin are "twin outcomes of a single underlying experience"—not influencing each other, but both being "used" by the same entity/drive (the "Dreamer"). He lays out eight systematized propositions:

1. Two types of time: lineal (mere process) and orthogonal (ontological, deepening layers of Being).
2. The "Dreamer" wants us to believe in the reality of lineal phenomena.
3. "Wachet auf!" (Johann Sebastian Bach's *Cantata 140*) — the command to awaken rolls back accretions of lineal time.
4. A "Greater Year" lasting thousands of years governs species-level cycles.
5. The period 200 BC – 1974 AD constitutes *Winter* in this Greater Year (slavery, pain, hopelessness).
6. The orthogonal axis is now rotating into *Spring* (evidenced by the fall of tyrannies).
7. The restoration of Moisture vs. desiccation (Dionysos vs. Set, the Egyptian death god).
8. We are Runciter's half-lifers in *UBIK*: neither fully alive nor dead—slumbering roots under snow, waiting for Spring.

He concludes with a reference to *Cantata 4* appearing in his dreams, and Bach's "Wachet auf" as the command of God to awaken from half-life.

## key_claims
- "We do not dream; we are dreamed." — Dick proposes an Active Dreamer who constructs both sleeping and waking reality.
- Lineal time (200 BC – 1974 AD) equals Winter in an orthogonal Great Year.
- Our reality contains hologram-like false segments filling in gaps.
- Spring 1974 is the macro-cosmic awakening identical to seed germination.
- *UBIK*'s half-life is "an accurate dramatic representation of our true state of Being."

## recurring_concepts
- Orthogonal time vs. lineal time
- The Dreamer
- Half-life (from *UBIK*)
- Spring / Winter / Great Year
- Moisture / Dionysos vs. Desiccation / Set
- Holographic false reality
- Bach's Cantatas 4 and 140

## scholarly_value
This is the single most developed theoretical statement in the early Exegesis. The eight-point schema prefigures almost the entire conceptual apparatus of *VALIS*. The Le Guin letter is also important for literary-historical reasons: it shows Dick trying to recruit a prominent SF author into his cosmological framework by arguing their fictions share a metaphysical origin. The fusion of Bach, Taoism, pre-Socratic philosophy, and SF tropes is quintessentially Dickian.
