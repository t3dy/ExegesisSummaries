# SUMMARY: SECTION_002
## March 2, 1975 — Letter to Phyllis Boucher

A brief, warm personal letter to Phyllis Boucher (Tony Boucher's widow). Dick updates her on his life in Fullerton, CA, his young son Christopher, his wife Tessa, and several professional developments: a piece for *The Real World* (Sufi magazine), Tessa's published poem, a *Rolling Stone* interview, and his experimental novel *Confessions of a Crap Artist*. He asserts that even his experimental/literary fiction remains science fiction—he will not deny the genre. Underneath the social pleasantries, Dick expresses a quiet religious conviction: "Our Good Friend is watching to reach out and scoop us up, when we fall behind."

## scholarly_value
This section provides key biographical anchoring (Fullerton address, Tessa, Christopher ~1.5 years old) and reveals Dick's relationship to the literary establishment circa 1975. His refusal to "go Vonnegut's route" and deny SF is a significant statement of genre identity.
