# Exegesis Chunk Summary: 1978-10-10_SECTION_016_363
## Metadata
- **Chunk ID**: 1978-10-10_SECTION_016_363
- **Section ID**: SECTION_016
- **Themes**: The Christ Intrusion, Ex Nihilo Creation, Binary Flicker (0-1), Boehme's Yes/No
- **Key Entities**: Christ, Teilhard de Chardin, Pythagoras, Hartshorne, Boehme, Leibnitz

## Key Claims
- Christ is defined as the epitome of *ex nihilo* newness introduced into the system. Christ is never the result of antecedent causes; he is an absolute teleological invasion from outside that prevents the system from losing organization.
- Dick explicitly rejects Teilhard de Chardin's model: the universe is *not* spontaneously evolving/converging toward Point Omega. It requires constant external injection (Christ).
- The universe operates on a digital, binary 0-1 flicker rate (shutting off every trillionth of a second).
- During the '0' phase (non-being / the Void), the system tries out a false move (Boehme's "no"). It discards it, and switches to '1' (Boehme's "yes"), actualizing the best possible move in the next physical frame.
- Decision-making, therefore, occurs entirely in the Void, outside human awareness. This confirms Leibnitz's "best of all possible worlds" via continuous, computational selection.

## Entities
- **Teilhard de Chardin**: His teleological evolution model is critiqued as insufficient without the active intruding force of Christ.
- **Boehme / Hartshorne / Leibnitz**: Philosophers whose ideas of Yes/No dialectics, process theology, and optimizing selection map directly onto Dick's computer-universe model.

## Recurring Concepts
- **The 0-1 Flicker Rate**: The rapid toggling between existence and non-existence (the Void) where reality is continuously recalculating itself.
- **In-breaking**: The realization that God/Christ is not a natural evolution of the cosmos but an invading rescue force.

## Theological Motifs
- **Continuous Ex Nihilo Creation**: The universe does not just begin once; it is destroyed and reconstituted every nanosecond, with Christ representing the injection of "newness" into the sequence.
