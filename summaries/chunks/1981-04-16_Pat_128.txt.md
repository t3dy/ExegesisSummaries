## Key Claims
- The system of reality is in absolute control and actively utilizes the disbelief of humanity. True moral action can only occur when people doubt the system; if they knew of the guaranteed reward (salvation), action would not be free or moral.
- Dick aligns his theological discoveries precisely with the Protestant theology of Paul and Martin Luther, updated through the 20th-century existentialism of Paul Tillich.
- Salvation requires man to take the primary, existential step: he must make a free-will choice to call out to God. This call is predicated on the absolute realization of his own total powerlessness and worthlessness.
- "Faith" is not a mere belief in doctrine; it is an active, existential cry for help based on an ontological crisis. Only when man makes this existential choice does God respond with Justification.

## Entities
- **Paul Tillich**: Modern existential Protestant theologian whose framework (existential choice, ultimate concern) perfectly matches Dick's understanding of the 1974 event.

## Recurring Concepts
- **The Paraclete**: God responding to man's existential cry for help by sending the Holy Spirit to act as advocate, comforter, and savior.

## Theological Motifs
- **Total Depravity and Total Reliance**: Man must recognize his total inability to save himself before he can rely entirely on God's grace—a core tenet of Reformation theology.
