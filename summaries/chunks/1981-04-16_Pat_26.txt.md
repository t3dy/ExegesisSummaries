## Key Claims
- Dick defines the world-order ("Kosmos") in Pythagorean terms: dynamic structure and mathematical harmony, rather than material substance.
- He clarifies that the world-order is *not* God. God is the Potter; the universe is the vessel. We can only know God inferentially via the "pressure" (Tao) He exerts to shape the vessel.
- He refines his understanding of the Maze: we generate the Maze through the very act of perceiving it. By fleeing our fate (annihilation), we generate linear time, which allows the Maze to grow in complexity and trapping power.
- **The Maze Frozen**: To escape the Maze, one must cease fleeing and face fate. This abolishes linear time, "freezing" the Maze into a static, spatial array (Plato's Forms). Once the Maze is frozen, it can be navigated and escaped.
- **The Grail**: Escaping the frozen Maze equates to finding the Grail. The "blood" in the Grail is the living information (plasmate) fired from outside by VALIS to guide the escapee.

## Entities
- **Pythagoras**: Referenced for defining reality as structure and the "harmonious fitting-together of the beautiful."
- **Eriugena**: Cited regarding God's radical transcendence—that God may not even be an object of knowledge to Himself.

## Recurring Concepts
- **The Potter and Vessel**: A classic metaphor repurposed to distinguish between God (the shaper) and the Reality Field (the shaped).

## Theological Motifs
- **Ch'ang Tao**: Dick equates Whitehead's God (the principle of selection) with the Tao, defining God as the invisible pressure that guides reality's evolution.
