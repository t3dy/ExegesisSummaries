## Key Claims
- In a massive epistemological reversal, Dick considers that his 1974 Christian visions were actually instances of *Maya* (Brahmanic illusion/magic). Because he believed in Christianity, Maya "obligingly" altered reality to confirm his beliefs.
- Therefore, the 1974 transformations (seeing ancient Rome, palm trees, Christian fish signs) do not prove Christianity is literally true; they prove Shankara and Mahayana Buddhism are right about the plastic, illusory nature of the phenomenal world.
- He was completely under the spell of illusion in 1974, but the very fact that reality *could* be molded like a conjurer's trick (the rope appearing as a snake) reveals the true, underlying metaphysical structure.
- He ultimately sets aside these vast epistemological puzzles, declaring them secondary. The true breakthrough is the pragmatic, living compassion of Tagore (the Buddha) caring for the ecosphere, finding this superior to endless theological abstractions.

## Entities
- **Adi Shankara**: His Advaita Vedanta theory of Maya (using the metaphor of a rope mistaken for a snake) perfectly describes how Valis manipulated the sensory environment in 1974.

## Recurring Concepts
- **The Plasticity of Reality**: The concept (first seen in *Ubik*) that objective reality is merely a projection or assumption that can be rewritten instantly by a higher power.

## Theological Motifs
- **Buddhism Subsuming Christianity**: Dick concludes that the Christian "magic" of 1974 is neurologically/epistemologically explained by the broader Asian concepts of Maya and the unenlightened illusion of form.
