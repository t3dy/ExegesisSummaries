# Exegesis Chunk Summary: 16-363

## Metadata
- **Chunk ID:** 16-363
- **Themes:** The Binary Void, The VALIS Paradox, Theophany vs. Mysticism.

## Key Claims
- **Binary Decision-Making:** The universe is "destroyed and recreated" every trillionth of a second. The "0" phase (Void/Non-being) is when the system tries out faulty solutions (Boehme's "no") and substitutes the "best possible" solution (the "1" phase/Boehme's "yes").
- **Unaware Thinking:** God's "thinking" takes place during the "off" phase (the Void), which is why we are normally unaware of the decision-making process.
- **The VALIS Koan:** The book *VALIS* is a Zen-like paradox. By claiming "I am a madman who saw Christ," the narrator creates an infinite regress that "points" to a truth that must arise *ex nihilo* in the reader’s mind (Satori).
- **Theophany vs. Mysticism:** 11-17-80 was a subjective "mystical experience" (altered state), whereas 2-3-74 was an "objective theophany" (God seen empirically in the world as "set to ground").
- **Moksa:** 3-74 represents the Indian concept of *moksa* (liberation), revealing God's "in-breaking" into an otherwise Eastern (Brahmanic) static field.
- **The Black Iron Prison Vision:** The apocalyptic vision was a "reward and a promise," showing the BIP and the secret Christians to confirm that victory would occur (Nixon's fall).

## Entities
- **Jacob Boehme:** His "Yes/No" dialectic is the basis for the 0-1 computer model.
- **Lord Krishna:** Invoked for the "tenth section of the Gita" where God is seen as the "cunning of the dice player."
- **Zeno/Sophists:** The practitioners of "paradox as knowledge."

## Recurring Concepts
- **Brahmanism:** Seeing God as the underlying substance of all things.
- **Flicker-Binary Pulsation:** The high-velocity "refresh rate" of reality.
- **Set to Ground:** God perceived as a distinct pattern *within* the world field.

## Theological Motifs
- **Satori:** Abrupt comprehension triggered by the collapse of logic.
- **Abba:** The Father’s nature as unconditional Agape discovered in 11-17-80.
- **Moksa:** Liberation through the correct perception of the theophany.
