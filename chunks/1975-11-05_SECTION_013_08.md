# The Fable of the Golden Groundsquirrel
## concise_summary
Dick creates a poignant animal allegory—the "Golden Groundsquirrel"—to represent his predicament and salvation. He equates himself to the squirrel trapped by "Romans" and saved by a "gigantic mysterious figure" (Christ/Daimon) who was once a squirrel and still carries the trauma of the crucifixion.
## key_claims
- Allegory of the Trap: Dick (the squirrel) was in a "trap" (political/personal) and saved by a figure (Christ) who restored him to his "familiar pasture."
- Ancestral Connection: The helper used "Koine Greek," proving he was once a "squirrel" (human) who was "glorified and transformed" (resurrection).
- Trauma of the Cross: The "gigantic figure" (Christ) still fears the Romans and "traps," reflecting the enduring trauma of his own crucifixion and empathetic bond with captives.
- Metamorphosis: The squirrel extrapolates that he, too, will eventually become a "mysterious gigantic figure" after death.
- Realism of the Vision: The vision of the "Promised Land" (Palm trees/Holy Land) was a metamorphosis of perception back to an "ultimate form."
- Plato's *Timaeus*: Identifies the "Steersman" (Demiurge/Reason) as the force of "Divine Intelligence" prevailing over "Ananke" (necessity/chance).
